The Circumgalactic Medium (CGM) is a diffuse, chemically diverse, multiphase gas located between the galactic disk and the halo's virial radius. Previous studies suggest that magnetic fields in the CGM evolve because of turbulent dynamo action, eventually reaching saturation in late-stage evolution. However, the mechanisms driving this process remain unclear. In this paper, we investigate the role of turbulence-driven feedback in shaping magnetic field evolution in the CGM of a simulated Milky Way (MW)-type galaxy using three MAIHEM simulations, two with 1.0 and one with 0.3~Z$_{\odot}$. We analyze cases with and without artificial feedback based on turbulence. Our results reveal that feedback induces late-stage magnetic field saturation after initial oscillations, while in the no-feedback case, the magnetic field exhibits a cubic growth. As turbulence evolves, dynamo action generates a co-rotating thick disk structure at the galaxy's center with a radius of $\sim$75 KPC. The early-stage disk instability in the feedback case causes magnetic field lines to align perpendicularly with the angular momentum vector, altering rotational dynamics and triggering explosive outflows of cold, highly magnetized gas to larger radii. These outflows promote a more isotropic CGM, leading to saturated magnetic fields in the later stages.
